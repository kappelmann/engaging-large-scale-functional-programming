Tax Avoidance Scheme

Your city has elected a new mayor - Mr Goldbach - who is a big fan of prime numbers. Not only does he call himself “The Prime Mayor”, but he also passed the following new tax law as a part of his prime policy:

Each citizen with income \(a\) coins has to 1. partition their coins into subsets such that each set contains a prime number of coins and 2. write down this partitioning in their tax declaration and pay exactly the number of paritions.

Now of course, all the extremely expensive, legacy tax finance programs (compatible only with Internet Explorer <= 9) of the republic are not worth a dime anymore. You, a young citizen with incredible maths abilities and a captilistic urge, realised that this indeed is your prime time to take over the market.

You want to write a novel piece of software, powered by Haskell, that makes everyone pay the least amount of tax under the new regulations. In a next step, you will create some marketing compaign, design a website, etc. but let’s stick to the software for now.

Input/Output Description

Input

A single integer \(a\): the income of a citizen.

Output

One integer: the minimum amount of tax the citizen must pay.

Constraints

  • \(2 \le a \le 10^{10}\)

Hint

Goldbach’s conjecture holds for all numbers that will be tested, that is any even \(n > 2\) is the sum of two primes.

Examples

Input

  1. 27
  2. 13
  3. 15
  4. 44
  5. 12342

Output

  1. 3
  2. 1
  3. 2
  4. 2
  5. 2
